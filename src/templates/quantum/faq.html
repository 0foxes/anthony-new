These are the most common questions worldwide about quantum computing.

What is quantum computing anyway?

Most computers only have 2 states at the most basic level; 1 and 0, on and off, yes and no. Those states can be interpreted in many ways, but each individual part is one or the other. This two-state representation is called binary. Quantum computers promise to change that by allowing each component to represent an infinite number of states. Although still in development, experts can already predict that they will help us secure data as well as present challenges to existing cryptography methods. Additionally, they have the potential to improve scientific simulations from the weather to drug research. This is because the universe is essentially a quantum (and not a binary) system, so quantum computers are more suitable for simulating it.

How important will quantum computing be?

Many technological innovations are initially dismissed as impossible or useless. Other innovations are hotly anticipated for the near future but gradually fizzle out. Quantum computing is not either of those. There have been many breathless predictions made, although some are more credible than others. The general scientific consensus seems to be that quantum computing will be very useful in the future, but the experts disagree on when exactly "the future" is. Quantum computers can already do certain computations that their non-quantum counterparts cannot, but have yet to actually do anything groundbreaking. However, other quantum technologies explored during the development of quantum computing development have been very useful. For more information, see the later answer on quantum key exchange.

Will quantum computing help AI?

This is an understandable question as both have been heavily featured in the media. Unfortunately, quantum computing and AI are mostly unrelated besides the fact that both are from the technology industry. This is to say that they may indirectly help each other by increasing public attention to technology, but they have no other relationship. On the flip side, this means that bright scientists interested in technology generally have to pick between one anda the other—so the industries compete for talent as well. An interesting anecdote is that Scott Aaronson (a quantum researcher) says that in AI practice is ahead of research but in quantum research is significantly ahead of practice.

Will quantum computing break encryption/cryptocurrency/Bitcoin?

These three are really all variants of the same question. Encryption protects data. Both people and businesses use encryption. All cryptocurrencies use encryption in one form or another. Bitcoin is one such cryptocurrency. As previously mentioned, quantum computers have the potential to break existing encryption algorithms. In many cases this is because the algorithms rely on the fact that if you take two large prime numbers p, q and multiply them to get n = pq, it is very hard to get p and q back from n even if you have a supercomputer. This process is called factoring. Quantum computers can use a process called Shor's algorithm to quickly do this. Although existing quantum computers (as of 2025) are not yet powerful enough to do this, many experts have raised concerns that future models could. This presents risks to anyone using encryption, which includes cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin.

How far away are quantum computers from breaking encryption?

This question is very hard to answer. For one, we only know the public state of quantum computing development. Countries or companies could have secret quantum computers more powerful than ones we know of. Additionally, some computer scientists have proposed that today's quantum computers are already very close to breaking encryption if we combine them with classical (non-quantum) computers. Finally, many nations and cybercrime groups are already collecting encrypted data so they can store it for years and later decrypt it with quantum computing. The good news is that most experts agree that quantum computers should not be able to break encryption today or within the next 2-3 years.

What are governments doing about this?

Broadly speaking, governments around the world are searching for encryption algorithms that do not rely on prime numbers, and will thus withstand attacks by quantum computers (as far as we know...). On one level, this means funding research grants so scientists are paid to research and develop those algorithms. Once algorithms are developed, government agencies (NIST of the United States for Western countries, GOST of Russia for Russia and central Asian countries) hold competitions. Scientists submit their algorithms, NIST invites other scientists to attack them, and a council of experts eventually decide on which algorithms to recommend. This process is called "standardisation" and every popular encryption algorithm has gone through it. NIST first began this process in 2016 and has already published some standards for quantum-safe encryption in 2023 and 2024.

What can I do about this?

For one, you may wish to join the quantum industry if you are young or looking for a change in career. Apart from that, most people are not equipped to research ways to defend computers against quantum computing. Nevertheless, you can write to your political representatives to explain the importance of quantum computing funding. Additionally, if you work for a company in e-commerce, healthcare, and banking that must keep data secure for long periods of time, you should be looking into quantum-safe encryption algorithms.

How can quantum technologies help me be more secure?

Previously, I mentioned that quantum technologies can help us be more secure. I'll skip explaining the physics, but the key point is that two people communicating could use a protocol known as "quantum key exchange" to both agree on a secret key to secure their messages with—even if a third person was listening in the whole time! After the secret key is established, no further eavesdropping is possible. This security is guaranteed by physics as we know it. While most people can't go out to buy lasers and satellites to use this, this technology has been heavily tested in the real world (including at UWaterloo) and banks and militaries have already started using it.
